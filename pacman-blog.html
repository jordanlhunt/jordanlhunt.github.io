Overview

Hello Internet! I wrote an approximate reconstruction of a PAC-MAN game in C using SDL2. My project isn't wholly a 1-to-1 arcade cabinet recreation, but it gets most of the fundamentals down. It was a fun and insightful project, and it helped me complete something from zero. My goal is to get a job in the games industry and the common advice I hear from independent game developers and computer programmers alike is to “make games”.

This project was totally self-guided, often misguided, and free of generative-AI usage. It was a project by me, for me, and its many flaws and shortcomings are mine too. Learning to develop on my own was one of the primary objectives for this summer endeavor. The whole project was a learning experience meant to force me to buckle myself into my seat and write some C, do research, and commit myself to learning the process. It was also meant to help me learn to enjoy the process.

Overall, I think it went quite well and I'll share my thoughts in this blog. I also want to write more in general, so this will be an excellent way to practice communicating my thoughts and experiences.

What Inspired Me to Pick This Project

As is common with many computer programmers, I got interested in writing code through playing games. I grew up playing games on the family computer along with reading comic books, manga, and sci-fi novels. I also played tabletop games like HeroClix and MechWarrior: Dark Age and tabletop role-playing games like Pathfinder, Traveller, D20 Modern, and many of the White Wolf games. I played some Magic: The Gathering as well; I was always fond of Blue decks. Of the countless games I have played, the ones that made me want to learn how to make games were the marvelous Cave Story+ and Bastion in 2011.

Those two games really made me understand that video games could be both entertaining and also tell stories that really touched the player's heart. Before and since, I have played games that I loved and that impacted me, but something about playing those two games made me understand that games were made by people, and I could be a person who made games. Those games were released during a time when online distribution and the very idea of an indie game were novel, and that really made me think "hey, I could make a game. I have a cool idea...".

(For the record that "cool idea" has not come to fruition because it's not a "cool idea", and I still lack the skills to make my "dream game" come to life.)

Fast-forward to today: I'm a 35-year-old man going back to school to get a computer science degree in the age of generative AI with hopes to get a job in the rapidly crumbling industry of video games. So after taking Computer Architecture in the spring and seeing C++ in action with a really great professor who really inspired me to more actively engage with my learning, I decided I needed a summer programming project. I wanted to do something outside my current ability level and something that would force me to learn something.

My Programming Background

I actually have a previous bachelor's degree in "Information Studies" from my first attempt at university. I genuinely cannot tell you what I learned in that degree program as I don't even know what it means. I essentially failed out of computer science my first time and I was lucky enough to have an advisor who really cared about student success. She helped me scrape my credits together and bundle them into a degree so I could graduate in a timely fashion. I did a little bit of Python and Java in school but nothing larger than a homework program that did some I/O and a for loop or two.

I didn't really do much programming there either; it was a very small amount of JavaScript my first year and a moderate amount of SQL. I also learned to use a proprietary software package I will never see again. I quit because I wasn't going anywhere or doing anything, and most importantly I was miserable.

All of my programming experience came from a couple of e-books on XNA/MonoGame and a random smattering of online tutorials. When I first tried to learn game development it was "XNA" which later became the "MonoGame framework" that you see today. I originally picked books on XNA because I learned that Bastion was written in XNA. I would later play "Salt & Sanctuary," another excellent game also written in XNA. The XNA/MonoGame books are quite good and I'd highly recommend anyone interested in learning game development pick up MonoGame. It's really beginner-friendly and the online community is nice and fun.

I always felt like I "wanted" to make games, but never had the drive to really learn the "process" of making games. I liked "wanting" more than I liked "trying" and I feel like this is a common behavior among a particular type of person who grew up with art and games but never quite made anything.

Why I Didn't Originally Proceed with Game Development as a Hobby

The biggest issues in my programming background were primarily two things: lack of self-confidence in my programming ability and a lack of self-motivating discipline. Those two issues created a negative feedback loop that really hindered my ability to do anything outside of a very direct tutorial. This was problematic because those two issues made getting better really hard. You can't get better as a programmer if you don't have the discipline and self-motivation to try to solve harder problems, and you can't improve your discipline if you don't try to do anything outside your comfort zone. It's a vicious cycle that kept me weighted down and limited my horizons.

I read just about every magazine article (remember when those were a thing?), every blog, and I watched every developer interview on "how to be an indie developer" and all of them said "just make games". The "just" part was my biggest issue, I couldn't seem to "just" do something. I never really believed I could do anything so I never even tried. I didn't quite understand that trying and failing is an essential part of trying and succeeding.

I didn't try hard enough mostly because I didn't want to fall short of my expectations. I was very tied to the outcome of things rather than the process. I think it was ultimately an elaborate form of procrastination to avoid disappointment. I don't know the exact reason but I spent far too much of my time in front of the computer not believing in myself. I did this project to prove to myself that I can do something -- even something hard and unknown. I also want to give myself a chance to make something that wasn't perfect. I also hope that anyone who may read this also internalizes the fact that they too can do something, and that there is meaning and dignity in trying to do something.

I think I worked through these limiting beliefs. At least enough for now. I know now I can do things, so long as I try hard and don't fight myself the entire time. I think this is true for anyone. I think we can all do great things.

About the Project

I recently read the book Masters of Doom and it was a really good history lesson on the nature of game development (at least a single slice of it). I was really impressed that DOOM was written in C. I had also always been a bit scared of C. My Computer Architecture course had us write in C and C++ and it was really refreshing. John Romero also mentions he really liked PAC-MAN in his DOOM Guy autobiography so I chose the arcade classic of yesteryear.

I had done some C and C++ tutorials in previous ventures so I thought it would be a good reason to restart where I originally began. I did some of the LazyFoo SDL2 tutorials in the past but again nothing outside the tutorial guidelines. These tutorials are quite good and I'd recommend them as an intro to SDL. I also wanted to do something in the old-school sense and just to start from zero. I elected not to use generative AI software or tools for this project as well.

I wanted to focus on learning, and I believe that learning comes from the mistakes you make and the struggle of trying to figure something out. This is the least glamorous part of learning and the part I think most online videos and writing seem to skip over. I think that, in the long term, this choice will benefit me as a developer. It may slow me down and may not make me as marketable, but I do think it will help me be a better computer science student.

I didn't do this project in one sitting. I didn't do this project on my first try. I didn't create a perfect recreation, and that's fine. I struggled on a lot of things and had to look up a lot, but that was the goal so I see that as a success.

Getting Lucky With the Dossier

When I picked a PAC-MAN clone, I knew I'd have to do some research on the game. During my initial research I discovered the Pac-Man Dossier, and it served as my design document. Strangely enough, I discovered the Pac-Man Dossier through the Game Developer magazine website. This webpage was a treasure trove of information both on the game and a great history lesson about it.

The dossier has a lot of great information. It had score values, behavioral explanations, and the exact details on the game I needed to recreate it. It was also something I could reference for what to research. Searching for programming or software help these days isn't that great. There is a lot of ad-driven information or engagement-driven information that I think pollutes the internet. I modified my uBlock filters to get rid of bad search results, and I had to acquire YouTube extensions to filter out the many unrelated search results.

I would read something from the dossier, run into something I didn't know how to do, and then do an internet search for an example. Many of the examples I'd find were not in C but in Java or Python which then led me to research how to do the same thing in C. This was not a completely optimal workflow, but it did force me to read a lot of code that wasn't mine. I believe that reading other people's code is a great way to improve my own.

The dossier included a lot of the inner workings of PAC-MAN and saved me a lot of time on figuring out what to do, but it wasn't a how-to guide. This project helped me improve my ability to implement an idea. The dossier would explain what I needed to do, and often why I needed to do it, however it was my responsibility to figure out how to connect those ideas of "what" and "why" into a "how".

I downloaded SDL2 and SDL3 from the website and VSCodium did most of the C/C++ configuration for me. I set up my Git repository and I was pretty much all set.

I tried using the classic Linux editors like emacs and vim, but I couldn't quite get the hang of it. What I Actually Learned During Development in a Technical Sense

Headers

This was my first C project that had multiple files. I once did an assignment in community college that had one or two shared headers but that was it. As my project grew I found that there was a lot of code that needed to know where other code was. I had multiple instances of circular dependencies throughout my development process. The biggest culprit was gamecontext.h, ecs.h, and main.h. I think this was because those are the most involved pieces of code I have.

I liked using headers because they force you to create and think about function signatures and to declare constants ahead of time. It's sort of like pseudocode in that you don't quite know how something ought to be implemented but it gives you a general shape most of the time and you can easily modify it. Having done this project in C, I found it to be very particularly insightful in that C doesn't do a lot of things for you. I was forced to learn about how forward declarations work and to ensure my .h files were populated with the proper data. Making sure that function signatures match in their .c and .h definitions is important and I goofed that up often.

Separation of Concerns

During my first year at MEGACORP, in my naïve enthusiasm to be a good employee I read a small amount of Head First Design Patterns because it was available in the employee "library". The "library" being a bookcase populated with full-throated Corpo propaganda, trite entrepreneur books, mid-90s MBA dreck, and religious conversion pamphlets. The design pattern book caught my eye by chance and I read it at my desk.

I learned about "separation of concerns" from that book and I had seen and read that design pattern mentioned in many of the game development tutorials as well. I tried to divide up as much as possible to make things do one thing or only handle one thing. Doing or handling one can be a rather broad statement but I think I managed to keep things small enough that the scope and context of any particular file could stay in my head.

main.c for example handles the game loop. Handling the game loop is main's job but handling the game loop means it has to coordinate a lot different things. Things like the gamecontext and the sdlcontext and all the various systems I ended up building. main had to handle all of those things but that meant all of those things themselves had to handle other things themselves.

I like to think of separation of concerns a bit like toy blocks: each concern is a block you bind and bundle blocks together to create small chunks of the whole set. With main being the whole playset that contains all of the smaller sets you had built prior. Also much like a toy block set, it can be difficult to see how a single piece fits into the whole until you actually put it all together. During the very beginning of my project I started with just map.h, maptile.c, and maze.txt. It's difficult to see how all of that will build into a larger game, but you can much more easily see how maptiles can be generated from the .txt file and be bundled together to create a map.

Entity Component System

The Entity Component System (ECS) is what I'm actually most proud of in this project. I first heard of an ECS from the Game Programming Patterns book I bought a long time ago. I read a little bit before but I never read it cover to cover. I'm currently reading it now as part of my mental recalibration toward reading more physical books every month. I don't rightly know if my project really needed an ECS, but it was really fun to research what an ECS is, how an ECS can be used for my particular project, and implementing it through trial and error.

Making things an Entity that's defined by various IDs made thinking about the types of systems I needed a lot easier. I had POSITION, VELOCITY, RENDERABLE component IDs, so it made me think I would need things like a movementsystem, collisionsystem, renderSystem and so on. Having an ECS made features like frightened mode, fruit spawning, and dot scoring easier to manage, since they’re just component changes or new entities rather than special‑case if‑chains.

This allowed me to focus more on "Data-Oriented Design" which is something I hear/see often online to be a superior methodology to "Object-oriented programming". I don't know if I was doing it correctly but I did see the value of data-oriented programming.

Defensive Programming

I have never created anything particularly large in C and I didn't know how easy it is to write code that "build" but doesn't run or runs but crashes as soon as you do something. Segmentation faults are a common trope online but I didn't realize how real it was until I started this project. I made sure that NULL pointers were handled properly, either through an instant return or some sort of output to console. C doesn't have all of the sort of niceties in something you'd get in something like Java. There aren't a lot of conveniences like .length() so you have to keep track of array bounds yourself. Just little things like that made me code a bit more carefully. I actually enjoy that in the sense it forces me to actively think about those sorts of things. In a project like this where it's just me, I didn't want to develop bad habits so I spent some time reading Stack Overflow and programming-related subreddits to get general feel for what a C programmer shouldn 't do.

The Basics of Git

I used Git and GitHub to store my code that allowed me to work on my home PC and the University PC with relative ease. Learning to git add, git commit -m, git push, and git pull became pretty natural. I used the GUI in VSCodium mostly but I also did several operations through the CLI as well. I even created a separate branch for the entity component system. I thought it would be good way to try and develop something totally separate from the rest of the code. Once I got it to work I was able to just merge it back into the main branch. It was relatively easy to create and merge. I would imagine if this project were more complex or had more contributors it would be more important to track changes more rigorously but luckily I didn't have to worry about that at all.

Pointers aren't that complex anymore

For the longest time I thought pointers were super confusing and unwieldy curiosities of the C language. With this project I have come to appreciate their value. A pointer is an address in memory, and it refers to a value somewhere else. Dereferencing a pointer with & allows you to access the value at that memory address. That's it. Pointers are often a tricky thing for CS students (myself included most certainly) but using them more frequently gets you into a groove and it starts to become more natural. One of the things that tripped me up was remembering I wasn't always passing a pointer, sometimes I was passing the memory address of the value.

Other Design Patterns

I didn't do it intentionally but I did implement the Flyweight and Facade Patterns. The Flyweight Pattern is something I recognized from my previous Monogame/XNA development and using in my graphics.c seemed to make sense to me. I loaded the spritesheet of the PAC-MAN sprites and then reused it to draw every sprite draw call instead of loading a duplicate texture for each entity. I didn't know the name of the Flyweight pattern and I had never seen any other way of drawing sprites.

I also used the Facade Pattern another one I was unfamiliar with by name but I knew the general idea behind it beforehand. In my InitializeSDL() method it's the single entry point for a more complex set of instructions. I didn't know that was a design pattern until re-reading the Head First Design Patterns book and searching online for fundamental C programming software engineering recommendations.

The Importance of Iteration

One of, if not the most important things I learned in this project from a technical perspective is that programming is a very iterative process. A lot of my code and Git commit messages are full of messages saying something is "sorta" working or I "fixed" something multiple times. Ideally, I'd like to be able to fix things once and be able to move forward on something else, but until then I think programming on my own is about getting something working and then getting something correct later, and then getting it "right" finally.

I don't think it's critical to focus on getting everything right on my first try every time for everything when it comes to programming as I don't think that's a healthy way of looking at problem-solving. I think that's a holdover from our education system's test-taking approach, in that they are one and done all-or-nothing exams. Working on a personal project gives you time to make mistakes and learn from them at much lower stakes.

Making mistakes, addressing the mistakes, and iterating is I think the purest loop of any programming project. There is no avoiding it, and even trying to avoid it is costly in time and understanding. Learning to use GDB, Valgrind, and rubber ducking are all essential skills for programming. In the same way creative writing involves a lot of rewriting, programming involves a lot of iteration. Sometimes it's a sign that I am going in the wrong direction, my project had many of those instances, but more often than not, those were the moments when I learned the most.

What I Actually Learned During Development in a Moral Sense

The Internet Warps Your Senses

I think there has been some sort of narrative in programming circles that everything is super fast and if you're not hacking together something near instantly you're not really a programmer. I started this project on April 9th and I'm finishing this post-development writeup on July 9th. Part of me feels like three months from zero to a functional prototype is rather good, however, speed is really one of the primary measures of value in our modern development culture. I think that's foolish and we are rewarding the wrong things for the wrong reasons in general. I didn't make the killer-app in three months and that's fine. That was never my objective.

Getting Better at Something Is Very Boring

Much like any other skill, art, or craft, getting better at programming is very incremental and unremarkable. I know a lot of stuff I didn't know prior because of this project, however I can't just hack together a full arcade classic like Centipede or Bubble Bobble. I'll have to keep coding away and solving more problems to be able to put one of those games together from scratch.

Knowing How My Tools Work Helps Me Work

Knowing the basics of how my tools (All the various software I use) work makes them easier to use. Taking some time to learn hotkeys makes getting around VSCodium easier. Even just simple things like changing files with CTRL+P or going to a symbol with CTRL+T helps reduce the friction of coding. There is a lot of friction already in development and a lot of it is totally necessary, but I shouldn't have friction going from one page to another. I'm going to learn more hotkeys and other CLI tools to help my development for my next project.

What's Next

This whole post is meant to expound upon what I learned in this project. During the development of this project I tried to track everything I learned. I really think it's important to be able to expound upon what you have learned or done in order to truly internalize your learning. I will do the same thing on my next project.

I'll probably make another clone of another arcade game, probably Galaga. It's a year older than PAC-MAN and more mechanically complex. I think that will be a good way to take a step and a half outside my current skill set and comfort zone. That's where all the learning happens.

Thank you for reading all of this.
